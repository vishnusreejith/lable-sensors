What is a Sensor?

Think of a sensor like a detective. Its job is to notice a specific clue (like a change in temperature, or the presence of a chemical) and report back by changing in a way we can see‚Äîusually by changing color! Today, you'll be the scientist building these chemical detectives out of paper.

Experiment 1: The Food Freshness Sensor

You Will Need:

  • Red cabbage, filter paper, baking soda, lemon juice, vegetable oil, paintbrush.

Step 1: Make the Indicator Paper

Chop red cabbage and add hot water. After 15 mins, strain the purple juice. Soak your paper in this juice and let it dry. This is your pH-sensitive paper!

Step 2: Assemble the Sensor

Coat the dry paper with a thin layer of oil. On one end, paint a spot of lemon juice (acid). On the other, paint a spot of baking soda paste (base). Observe what happens over an hour.

Purple (Neutral)

How It Works:

Red cabbage contains a pigment called anthocyanin. This molecule changes its shape depending on the pH level (how acidic or basic something is). In an acidic environment, it turns pink/red. In a neutral one, it's purple. And in a basic (alkaline) environment, it turns green/blue. Your sensor works because the acid and base slowly mix through the oil, changing the pH and thus the color over time.

Experiment 2: The Secret Message Paper

You Will Need:

  • Thermochromic pigment (choose one that is **colorless/white at room temp** and becomes colored when warm)
  • Clear glue or clear acrylic paint base
  • Sturdy white paper (like cardstock)
  • Paintbrush & a mixing cup

Step 1: Create the Magic Paint

In your cup, mix a small amount of the thermochromic pigment powder with the clear glue. Stir until it's a smooth paste. It might look milky or white.

Step 2: Write Your Secret

Use your paintbrush to write a message or draw a picture on the paper with your magic paint. Let it dry completely. When dry, your message should be **nearly invisible**.

Step 3: Reveal the Secret

Firmly press your thumb onto the paper where you wrote. The warmth from your finger should make the secret message **appear in color**! A hairdryer on low heat will reveal the entire message.

How It Works: A Deeper Look

The magic is in thermochromism. Think of the pigment as having a special dye molecule that can be in two different states.

üßä When it's cold (room temp): The dye's chemical structure is in a shape that doesn't absorb light, making it **transparent or colorless**. Your message is hidden.
üî• When it gets warm: The heat gives the molecules energy to twist into a new shape. This new structure absorbs light in a different way, which allows it to **show its color**. The secret message appears!

This amazing technology is used to make all sorts of fun and useful things, like toys that change color in water or novelty mugs.

Experiment 3: The Invisible Gas Detector

You Will Need:

  • Red cabbage indicator paper, household ammonia, a large clear jar with a lid, a bottle cap, tape.

Procedure:

Tape the indicator paper inside the jar. Place a bottle cap with a tiny bit of ammonia at the bottom. Seal the jar and watch the paper. The invisible gas will change its color!

Ammonia Gas Simulation

Click the button to seal the jar and start the simulation. Watch the paper change color as the invisible gas spreads.

How It Works:

Ammonia is volatile, meaning it easily turns from a liquid into a gas. This ammonia gas spreads throughout the jar. When the gas molecules touch the indicator paper, they dissolve in the moisture of the paper and create a basic solution. This raises the paper's pH, causing the cabbage indicator to turn greenish-blue, detecting the gas you can't see!

Safety First! Always have an adult help you, especially when using ammonia or a hair dryer.
